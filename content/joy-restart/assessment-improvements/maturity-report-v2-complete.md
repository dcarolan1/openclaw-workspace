## YOUR JOURNEY — Where You Are and Why It Matters

Maturity doesn't follow a calendar. A person can be 50 years old and still be developing the relational skills that should have been learned at 5 — not because of failure, but because no one taught them. Our culture rewards independence, performance, and toughness. It rarely teaches us how to receive love, stay connected under stress, know who we are apart from our roles, or guide others from joy instead of duty.

That's why this report may feel surprising — or even hard to read. If your strongest stage is somewhere you didn't expect, or your growth area feels uncomfortably basic, that's not a verdict. It's clarity. And clarity is where God does His best work.

## GROWTH SUGGESTIONS

**Daily:**
When you feel the urge to withdraw or shut down, pause for 10 seconds before responding. Name what you're feeling — even silently. This interrupts the pattern and creates space for a different choice.

**Weekly:**
Have one honest conversation with a safe person about something that bothered you. Practice staying connected through it — not fixing it, not defending yourself, just staying present.

**Relational:**
Ask someone who knows you well: "Do I tend to pull away when things get hard?" Listen to their answer without defending. Their observation is data, not accusation.

**Spiritual:**
Spend 5 minutes daily in stillness, asking God: "Where are You inviting me to stay connected instead of withdrawing?" Listen for the still small voice — not for an answer, but for His presence.

**As a parent:**
Notice moments when you're parenting from depletion rather than joy. When you catch it, don't add guilt — add rest. Even 10 minutes of receiving care (from your spouse, a friend, or God directly) can shift the next hour of parenting from duty to delight.

### From 5 Stages to 8: Why We Expanded the Model

Dr. Jim Wilder's Life Model identifies 5 core stages of maturity: Infant, Child, Adult, Parent, and Elder. These stages describe the relational capacities God designed humans to develop — from receiving love all the way through spiritual wisdom.

At Joy Restart, we've expanded this to 8 stages. Not to replace the original — which remains foundational — but to capture how our modern Western culture fractures development within certain stages.

In most traditional cultures throughout history — First Nations, African, Asian, Middle Eastern — these stages were supported by community rituals, mentoring relationships, and clear expectations at each phase of life. In our culture, that support system has largely broken down. Children are pressured into adult responsibilities before they've learned to receive love. Teenagers form identities from social media instead of elders. Parents are promoted at work before they've developed empathy at home.

The result: people develop some skills within a stage while missing others. Our 8-stage model identifies exactly where within a stage someone may be stuck.

### The 8 Stages Mapped to the Life Model's 5

|---|------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Receiving Love** | Infant | Same — the foundation of all maturity |
| 2 | **Early Relational Skills** | Child (younger) | Child splits into two: early skills are about boundaries, play, exploration, and the freedom to try new things safely |
| 3 | **Relational Stability** | Child (older) | The second Child stage: staying connected when corrected, expressing needs calmly, handling disappointment without shutting down |
| 4 | **Identity & Values** | Teen/Adolescent | Added: our culture makes identity formation especially difficult. Many adults are trapped between childhood compliance and not knowing who they truly are |
| 5 | **Personal Responsibility** | Adult | Same — ownership, balance, choosing connection over being right |
| 6 | **Hands-on Care** | Parent (younger) | Parent splits into two: younger parenting is direct care of dependents — feeding, protecting, guiding, comforting |

### Healthy vs. Unhealthy Expressions of Trapped Stages

When a stage is "trapped" — meaning it wasn't fully developed at the expected time — it shows up in predictable patterns:

**Trapped at Receiving Love (Infant):**
- *Healthy expression:* Learning to receive love later in life, building trust slowly but intentionally with safe people
- *Unhealthy expression:* Chronic self-sufficiency, refusing comfort, guilt about needing others, inability to rest

**Trapped at Early Relational Skills (Child — younger):**
- *Healthy expression:* Rediscovering play, creativity, and exploration in safe adult relationships
- *Unhealthy expression:* Rigidity, fear of new experiences, inability to be spontaneous, over-control of environment

**Trapped at Relational Stability (Child — older):**
- *Healthy expression:* Recognizing the withdrawal pattern and practicing repair with a trusted friend or counselor
- *Unhealthy expression:* Shutting down after correction, silent treatment, explosive reactions to small disappointments, chronic conflict avoidance

**Trapped at Identity & Values (Teen/Adolescent):**
- *Healthy expression:* Actively exploring faith, values, and identity in safe community — asking hard questions without fear
- *Unhealthy expression:* People-pleasing, chameleon behavior (different selves for different audiences), not knowing what you actually believe vs. what you were told to believe, identity built entirely on roles or performance

**Trapped at Personal Responsibility (Adult):**
- *Healthy expression:* Learning to own mistakes later in life, developing the humility to say "I was wrong"
- *Unhealthy expression:* Blame-shifting, defensiveness, inability to balance self-care with caring for others, needing to be right

**Trapped at Hands-on Care (Parent — younger):**
- *Healthy expression:* Developing joyful, sustainable care practices through rest and boundaries
- *Unhealthy expression:* Burnout, resentment, over-functioning, martyr complex, caring for others while neglecting self

**Trapped at Empowering & Releasing (Parent — older):**
- *Healthy expression:* Gradually releasing control and trusting others with increasing responsibility
- *Unhealthy expression:* Micromanaging, inability to delegate, doing for others what they could do for themselves, feeling threatened when others succeed independently

**Trapped at Spiritual Wisdom (Elder):**
- *Healthy expression:* Growing into blessing, peace, and shared authority at a natural pace
- *Unhealthy expression:* Holding power, seeking recognition, inability to bless others' success, critical spirit, controlling through spiritual authority

### Historical and Non-Western Perspectives on Maturity

The maturity model draws from traditional cultures who recognize maturity as the experience of life itself — not achievement or credentials.

| Life Phase | Traditional Culture | Modern Western Culture |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Infant (0-3) | Community raises the child. Bonding is primary. | Often isolated nuclear family. Daycare from infancy. |
| Child (4-12) | Play, imagination, boundaries learned in community. Contribution to household. | Structured activities. Academic pressure. Limited free play. |
| Teen (13-19) | Many cultures mark adulthood with ritual by 13-16. Identity formed through elders and mentors. | Identity formed through peers and social media. Delayed maturity. Extended adolescence into mid-20s. |
| Adult (20-35) | Marriage, children, vocational identity. Full community membership. | Career focus. Delayed marriage. Identity through achievement. |
| Parent (35-60) | Guiding the next generation. Community leadership. | Often still establishing personal identity. "Midlife crisis" as symptom of incomplete earlier stages. |
| Elder (60+) | Wisdom keeper. Major community decisions. Mediation. Elevated status. | Often marginalized. "Retirement" as removal from contribution. |

These stages are not strictly age-locked, and transition can happen with or without ritual. Our cultural expectations, when they align with our maturity stage, support progress. When they don't — when a culture demands adult performance from a relational child — the fracturing begins. God's grace and redemption continue to work beyond these cultural milestones.
